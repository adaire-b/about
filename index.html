Adaire Baltzer

Workflow Designer • Systems Thinker

I get genuinely excited about solving problems with technology. I always have.

I'm happiest when I can use what I know to help someone else, especially if it gives them back time they didn't have before, offers a new perspective, or removes the friction that keeps them from doing their best work.

When I was deciding what to study, I thought I had to choose between teaching and computers. I chose teaching because I wanted to help people. Looking back, I don't think I ever left technology behind. The moments that energized me most were always the ones where the two came together.

I feel at home when I'm being productive on a computer. Whether I was building a spreadsheet, automating a repetitive task, organizing information, or teaching someone a new tool, I found myself asking the same question over and over:

There has to be a better way.

Over time, people started bringing me problems they didn't know how to solve. They'd joke that I was some kind of magician.

I'm not.

I just don't like broken things. I feel compelled to understand them, fix them, and leave them better than I found them. I'm simply using my talents to be useful, and that makes me happy.

The projects in this portfolio weren't created because I wanted to build software. They grew out of real frustrations, real conversations, and real people who deserved better tools. Education happened to be where I found those problems, but what has always excited me is understanding how people work and using technology to help them work better.

For years, I thought I had chosen teaching over technology. Looking back, I don't think I ever had to choose. I've always been happiest where the two meet: solving real problems, building useful tools, and helping people do their best work.
